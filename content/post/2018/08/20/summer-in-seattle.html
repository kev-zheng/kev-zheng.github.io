Here’s a post documenting my experience as a Software Development Intern at Amazon

my attempt at taking 1 second everyday

The Process

  • Applied online end of October, started process mid-november
  • Two online assessments, final round phone interview, offer by January
  • Team selection decision a month before start date

The Job

My project was to develop a service to handle validation of tax registration numbers - for example, numbers that Amazon collects from sellers to collect sales tax. These have have to be well-formed in order for Amazon’s systems to identify and calculate the correct taxes for purchases.

I was fortunate to have an impactful project to my team! I met with my team many times to go over design decisions, culminating in a comprehensive design document and design review with both my team the Hyderabad team.

A major part of my project included developing an encrypted blacklist. Similar to credit card numbers, customer tax registrations are critical data and must comply with strict regulations. I worked with several AWS services to handle encryption, validation, and storage of this blacklisted data, and took care to implement caching where useful to minimize database and encryption/decryption calls.

A typical day: Arrive 10 am, plan day to day tasks. Stand-up around 11 am. Code, lunch, code, with frequent mentorship meetings, leave around 5-7pm depending on pending work.

I enjoyed the style of work - I had plenty of opportunity to be independent and do my own research. I noticed that Amazon rewards independence - one especially important leadership principle is “Bias for Action” which my manager brought up frequently.

My mentor was busy but always willing to help, so as I worked, I would rack up a list of items that I was blocked on with my best decision to proceed. Often he would OK them, but many other times we would have to sit down and reconsider the project design

What I learned

Java

When I came in, I had no prior experience programming in Java. I gained new respect for the language since it’s much simpler to code in than C++ and has a rich community of open source libraries. I picked up design patterns, learned object oriented programming, as well as gained experience with dependency injection and testing/mocking frameworks.

Devops

Internal tools. Amazon has a unique build system that handles dependency resolution between thousands of services. Consequently, it’s also massive and slow to use, but I can appreciate how effective it is compared to a Makefile

Git. Using git at work is very different than at school, especially at Amazon. Commits are designed to be a distinct feature, and also short enough to be easily reviewed. By the end, I was well aquainted with developing on multiple branches and using rebase to edit commits

Agile. Development is spread across two-week sprints. At the beginning of each sprint, I identified tasks to focus on and estimated how long each one would took. Unsurprisingly, my estimates were off, and new problems appeared every week, but it is helpful to have a consistent board of tasks to work on day by day.

Design

Service oriented architecture.

Teams at Amazon are usually around 10 people, aptly called ‘two pizza teams’. Each team can take ownership of several services, effectively distributing the work.

I learned a lot in taking ownership of my own service, and was able to understand many design decisions involved in the interaction between services. In a way, it’s similar to how good C++ modules are designed, separating interface (.h) from implementatin (.cpp) while carefully selecting the dependencies between modules.

Security.

It’s cool to be able to work with real customer data, but this comes with the responsibility of handling it safely. Data has to be encrypted both in transit and at rest, and there are different tools (both internal and external, like AWS KMS) that can be used for both.

My project had large impact on the security of the overall system. Considering that validation is normally the only function that requires working with plaintext, callers can validate encrypted data and pass it around knowing that it’s safe to use.

Distributed systems.

Amazon operates across the globe. What happens when an American seller is in Europe? Deploying code to multiple hosts distributed across multiple continents presents new challenges, for example:

  • consistency (when will I see changes?)
  • latency (will it matter if I serve European users using a host in the US?)
  • geographical (is this service that I want to use available in China?)
